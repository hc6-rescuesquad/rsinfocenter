What are Fires?

Fire is the visible effect of the process of combustion â€“ a special type of chemical reaction. It occurs between oxygen in the air and some sort of fuel. The products from the chemical reaction are completely different from the starting material.
Fire generates a black, impenetrable smoke that blocks vision and stings the eye. It is impossible to navigate through suck smoke, so fire drill participants should practice evacuating houses/buildings by at least two routes.




Causes of Fires

  • Dead leaves
  • Faulty electrical wiring
  • Faulty electrical equipment
  • Unattended stoves and ovens
  • Unattended candles
  • Children playing with fire
  • Flammable liquid
  • Careless smoking


  • Dried Leaves - Remember to clean the areas in and around your house regularly. You should immediately dispose of dried leaves, wood shavings, and other items that may easily catch fire.
  • Faulty Electrical Wiring - To protect your family, hire a licensed electrician to check your electrical installations regularly. Ask them to repair or replace frayed or faulty wiring and fixtures.
  • Faulty Electrical Equipment - Appliances and gadgets should not be plugged in when not in use.
  • Unattended Stoves and Ovens - Always pay attention to your cooking. Place combustibles like paper towels and rags away from heat sources.
  • Unattended Candles - For a safe space, keep your candles on a sturdy holder on an even surface. Make sure that they are out of reach of children and pets and always blow them out before leaving the room.
  • Children Playing With Fire - Always keep your matches, lighters, and torches in places that the children cannot reach.
  • Flammable Liquid - Always store flammable liquids in approved containers in a cool, ventilated area outside your home.
  • Careless Smoking - Clean the ashtrays regularly and place them away from materials that burn easily.



The Fire Triangle

The Fire Triangle or Combustion Triangle is a simple model for understanding the necessary elements that start most fires. The triangle illustrates the three elements a fire needs to ignite: heat, fuel, and oxygen.
Fire is composed of three elements - heat, fuel, and oxygen. which when combined, will result in a chemical reaction called burning. The leading cause of death in a fire is asphyxiation (Choking Sensation). Inhaling carbon monoxide can cause loss of consciousness or death within minutes. The heat from a hostile fire exceeds anything to which a person is normally exposed. A fully developed room fire has temperatures over 1,100 degrees Fahrenheit

What to do?

Preventive Measures
  • Put phone numbers of the fire department near your land-line phone.
  • Do not smoke in bed, especially after drinking.
  • Eliminate fire hazards through good housekeeping. Dispose of waste paper, rubbish, and other flammable materials regularly.
  • Keep matches and lighters out of children's reach.
  • Do not leave cooking appliances running.
  • DO not use a broken plug or pull a plug out of an outlet by the cord as this may cause overheating and possibly even a fire.
  • Keep a First Aid Kit handy.

But what if there's a Fire?
  • We all try to prevent fire starting in our home. But it only takes an unguarded or careless moment for a fire to start. A couple of minutes later and your home could be filled with smoke. Smoke can fumes can kill - particularly the highly poisonous smoke from some furnishings. You will only have a short time to get out. Use it wisely and try not to panic.
  • If possible, close the door of the room where the fire is and close all doors behind you as you leave. This will help delay the spread of fire and smoke.
  • After the first sign of fire, make every attempt to put it out. The first 3 minutes is critical as it is still containable with fire extuingishers. Thereafter, the nearest fire alarm should be sounded.
  • CALL FOR HELP.

    And after?
  • Treat any injuries on yourself.
  • Report to the nearest authority.
